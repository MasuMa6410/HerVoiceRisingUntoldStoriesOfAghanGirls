Her Voices Rising

Untold Stories of Afghan Girls

This platform shares the voices of Afghan girls whose stories are often unheard. Through safe and respectful storytelling, we aim to highlight their real experiences, challenges, and strengths.

Our goal is to create understanding by sharing real voices, not just opinions. We believe that when stories are heard directly, they can build empathy and awareness.

Gurdaafarid’s Journey

Gurdaafarid, a young girl from northern Afghanistan, shares her journey of fighting for education and freedom despite social pressure and danger.

Gurdaafarid’s Journey

Gurdaafarid grew up in northern Afghanistan, in a place full of history and beauty, but also full of limitations for girls. In her community, girls were expected to stay silent, follow strict rules, and focus only on home life. But from a young age, she dreamed of something more, she wanted to study, to learn, and to build a different future. Her mother quietly supported her, even when others did not. With very little money, Gurdaafarid worked hard to pay for her education. She faced judgment, harsh words, and social pressure, but she did not stop. Step by step, she continued her studies, joined classes, and eventually earned a place at a local university. That moment felt like hope becoming real not only for her, but for many girls like her.But everything changed suddenly. When girls were no longer allowed to study, her dreams were taken away overnight. Schools and universities closed their doors to her. What she had worked for years to achieve was gone in a moment.Faced with this reality, Gurdaafarid made a difficult decision. She chose to leave her country not because she wanted to, but because she had no other way to continue her education. The journey was dangerous and uncertain. One night, she traveled toward the border, filled with fear and sadness, knowing she might not see her family again.With courage and the help of others along the way, she crossed into a new place. She had no clear path, no resources, and no guarantee of what would happen next. But she did not give up. After many struggles, waiting, and uncertainty, she was finally given a chance to continue her studies.Today, Gurdaafarid is studying at a university abroad. She carries her past with her, not as a burden, but as a reminder of why she continues. She now uses her voice to speak for Afghan girls who are still unable to share their stories. Her journey is not just about leaving it is about rising, rebuilding, and refusing to be silent. Gurdaafarid’s story is a powerful example of strength in the face of loss. It shows how even when dreams are taken away, the human spirit can find a way to keep going, to keep hoping, and to keep fighting for a better future.

Sahar’s Journey

Sahar, a young Afghan woman and volunteer teacher, shares her journey of continuing her education despite hardship and helping other girls learn.

Sahar’s Journey

Sahar spent part of her childhood as a refugee in Pakistan. Life there was not easy. Her family faced financial struggles, and daily life was full of uncertainty. But even in those conditions, Sahar did not give up on her education. With support from her father, she continued studying. She stayed focused on her goals, even when resources were limited and the future felt unclear. Step by step, she moved forward. Later, Sahar returned to Afghanistan and continued her studies. She successfully graduated from university with a degree in computer science. This was not just a personal achievement it was proof that persistence can lead to real change. Today, Sahar is using her knowledge to help others. She works as a volunteer teacher in Nangarhar, where she teaches girls computer skills and English. Many of her students are also refugees or girls who have faced displacement and challenges in their lives. Even though she does not receive a salary, Sahar continues her work with commitment. She believes that education is one of the most powerful tools a girl can have. She wants to be more than a teachershe wants to be an example. Through her work, she encourages girls to stay strong, to keep learning, and to believe in their future. She shows them that even in difficult situations, it is possible to grow and create new opportunities. Sahar believes that knowledge can open doors and she is helping others find those doors. Her story is a testament to the power of education and the strength of the human spirit. It reminds us that even when life is hard, we can still find ways to rise, to learn, and to help others do the same.

Rural Voices

Girls in rural areas often face additional challenges, including limited access to education and fewer opportunities to share their voices. These stories reflect both their struggles and their strength.

Laila’s Journey

Laila, a rural woman from Baghlan, shares her life of hardship, forced marriage, and her quiet hope for a different future.

Laila’s Journey

Laila lives in a remote desert area of Baghlan, where water is scarce and life is harsh. Her home, made of mud and straw, blends into the dry landscape around her. At just 30 years old, her face already shows the marks of a difficult life. For Laila, a normal life would include simple things clean water, education, and love. But none of these were given to her. The nearest water source is far away, and even that is not clean. Every drop she carries is important for survival. In her village, girls do not go to school. Their education is limited, and most are married at a young age. Laila was only 15 when she was forced into marriage with a man twice her age. She later learned that her father had accepted money in exchange for her marriage. Even though she knew this, she still hoped he would refuse. The marriage brought her pain from the beginning. She did not choose her husband, and she struggled emotionally and physically. During her engagement, she would become sick whenever she heard his name. On her wedding day, she felt fear instead of happiness. Over the years, Laila gave birth seven times, but five of her children did not survive. Each loss brought deep sadness. The repeated cycle of hope and loss made her life even more difficult. Despite everything, Laila had dreams. She always wanted to go to school and learn to read and write. She imagined herself wearing a white school uniform. But her husband did not allow her to study. He believed she wanted to escape. To cope with her situation, Laila learned embroidery. She became skilled and started making clothes for others. This small work gave her a sense of purpose, but even this created tension in her home, as her husband feared she might gain independence. Eventually, Laila decided she wanted to leave her marriage. She secretly saved money and made a plan to escape. But before she could act, the situation in the country changed again. With the return of the Taliban, her hopes of leaving disappeared. She was forced to stay. Now, Laila continues her daily life taking care of animals, doing housework, and living with a man she does not love. She has no access to education, no support system, and no safe way out. Yet, even in this situation, she has not given up completely. Deep inside, she still hopes for a simple, peaceful life.

A Voice Rising

She is a young girl from a rural area, sharing her journey of surviving a life-threatening moment and questioning why many others do not get the same chance to live.

A Voice Rising

My hand was sweating from fear, as if it had just been washed. My mother held it so tightly that it felt like she was handing me over to death. In that moment, everything felt uncertain. Instead of waking up after three hours, I remained unconscious for twelve. After six hours, my breathing stopped. The machine was turned off. The doctors removed their masks and quietly left the operating room. Only two nurses stayed, pushing the bed I was lying on. Outside, my mother was waiting. She called out with hope, “My daughter’s room is here!” But the doctors looked at her with heavy eyes and slowly shook their heads. They were telling her that I was no longer breathing. The nurses pulled the white sheet higher, trying to hide my face. The doctor spoke softly, “Please call her father. He should come, sign the papers, and take his daughter’s body.” But my mother refused to believe it. “No… my daughter is not weak. She is alive.” They began to move my body toward the hospital morgue on the lower floor. As they reached the stairs, something unexpected happened. The attendants suddenly stopped and shouted, “Doctor! Doctor! She is alive!” In that moment, I took a breath. The white sheet moved up and down with each breath I took. Everything became loud and chaotic. People ran back toward the operating room. Voices filled the space: “How is this possible?” “She came back to life…” I came back. But I keep thinking about those who never do. How many children lose their lives because help comes too late? How many never reach a hospital because it is too far away? How many families cannot afford treatment? In rural areas like mine, these stories are not rare. They happen again and again.

Zaynab’s Journey

Zaynab, a young girl from Kabul, shares her experience of trying to continue her education while living through uncertainty and fear after the Taliban’s return.

Zaynab’s Journey

Seven-year-old Zaynab lives in the suburbs of Kabul. Her life changed when the Taliban entered the city in August, taking control without resistance. Before this, Zaynab had only spent one year in school. She had heard about the Taliban, but she had never seen them. Recently, she watched a video on her neighbor’s phone. In the video, Taliban fighters were riding a bicycle, shouting, and singing. It was the first time she saw them, even if only on a screen. So far, the Taliban have not come to her area. Her father says life still feels “normal,” and all his children are still going to school. But this normal life feels uncertain. He watches the situation closely. He says if he feels unsafe, he will stop his children from going to school. “Zaynab doesn’t know the Taliban, but I remember them,” he explains. “I hope she never has to see what I have seen.” Even with this fear, he allows her to continue. “She loves school,” he says. “I don’t know how long this will last, so why not let her go for now?” Across the country, many families are facing the same difficult choices. Under Taliban rule in the past, girls were not allowed to study or work. Today, their position on girls’ education is still unclear. Some statements promise rights, but reports from different areas show mixed realities. For many girls, this uncertainty has already changed their lives. Samira, a 15-year-old girl living near Kabul, has stopped going to school. Her mother is afraid and does not allow her to leave the house. “Maybe if things calm down, I can return,” she says, but she does not sound sure. Her older sister, Nahida, has also stopped attending university. She is afraid of walking past Taliban members every day. Education in Afghanistan has depended heavily on international support. Over the past 20 years, millions of girls were able to go to school. But now, funding has been frozen, and the future of education is uncertain again. Even if schools remain open in some places, safety is still a major concern. Zaynab continues to go to school for now. She does not fully understand what is happening around her. But like many girls, her future depends on decisions far beyond her control.

hidden struggles

Not all challenges are visible. Many girls face silent struggles shaped by social expectations, limited opportunities, and lack of representation. These stories aim to bring those hidden realities to light in a respectful way.

The Night of Waiting

A young girl from Kabul shares her journey of living through loss, silence, and longing after being separated from her education and dreams.

The Night of Waiting

It is 11:30 at night. I am sitting on the rooftop, just like always, staring at the stars. I try to think positively, but tonight a deep pain fills my heart. It is not sharp it is slow and quiet, but it touches every part of me. It feels familiar, like it has been living inside me for a long time. I close my eyes and imagine myself in front of the mirror. I see happiness in my eyes. I am wearing my white headscarf and my clean school uniform. Those mornings, getting ready for school, were some of the most beautiful moments of my life. But when I open my eyes again, everything is dark. I am alone. My eyes fall on the old school building near our house. I remember the nights I would look at it from this same rooftop and make up scary stories. The next morning, I would tell them to my friends and warn them not to go to quiet places alone. It makes me smile now, thinking about how easily they believed me. Tonight, I miss those days. I miss the laughter without worry. It has been three years since I have been away from school. Three years of missing a life that once felt normal. I ask myself, was this my fault? Was it fair that I became part of a political struggle I never chose? I did not want to grow up this fast. Yes, I have become stronger and more realistic, but what about the other girls? The ones who were forced into early marriages, the ones who gave up on life, the ones who stopped dreaming. Sometimes I wonder, who understands this pain? Who holds my hand and says, “I understand you”? People remember history and mourn for the past, but they forget what is happening now. They forget the explosions, the losses, the broken hearts. There is a saying: one of the hardest feelings is missing someone who is still there. That is how I feel. I am still in Kabul, but Kabul is no longer in me. I try to calm my pain by writing, visiting graves, and listening to music. But sometimes words are not enough. I remember the explosion at the Kaaj Educational Center in 2022. For weeks, I felt lost and broken. My brother once told me that those who plan violence also think about how to break people’s hope. But they forget something important our strength. They may try to break us, but we continue. They took away our right to go to school, but we kept learning. They broke our pens, but we kept writing. They tried to silence us, but we kept dreaming. They wanted to turn our lives into darkness, but our dreams still have color. Tonight, I make a promise to myself again to continue, to stay strong. Because this is not just my story. These hands that write belong to many girls who can no longer write. I will continue for them. I promise that one day, no girl will cry because she cannot go to school. That she will live freely, with dignity, in her own country. We were the girls who stayed strong in the middle of destruction. Like flowers growing in difficult places, we survived. And survival like this has deep roots. The next generation must not forget us.

Gulnisa’s Journey

Gulnisa, a girl from a remote mountain village, shares her journey of forced marriage, violence, and her struggle to survive and protect her children.

Gulnisa’s Journey

Gulnisa was born in a small village deep in the Hindu Kush mountains. Her childhood was simple, filled with small moments and little understanding of the world beyond her village. But her life changed suddenly when she was forced into marriage. She was married to an older man, an army officer who already had a wife and children her age. Without choice or preparation, Gulnisa found herself in a life she did not understand. She became the second wife, living under tension, facing resentment from the first wife, and trying to care for children who did not accept her. Her marriage was not about her future. It was arranged by her family to protect her brother, showing how women were often used to solve problems between men. In her society, women had little voice, and decisions were made without their consent. Gulnisa did not even know about her marriage until the night of her henna ceremony. When she found out, she felt trapped and hopeless. In desperation, she tried to end her life by swallowing poison. She survived, but her marriage still went ahead in silence, without celebration. Life after marriage was filled with hardship. She lived in a system where women had no power, no education, and no right to work. Her story was not unique many women in rural areas faced the same reality. Later, she and her stepchildren moved to Kabul, hoping for a better life. But tragedy followed her again. Her husband was killed, and after his death, her stepchildren took control of everything. They forced her to give up her husband’s property, beat her, and even tried to harm her child. With no support and no safety, Gulnisa was forced out of her home. For some time, she lived in a cave near Kabul. Eventually, with the help of kind people, she found her way to a job in a hospital and began trying to rebuild her life. But the danger did not end. Her stepson found her again and attacked her. Soon after, the situation in the country changed once more. Armed groups searched her home, beat her, and threatened her because of her husband’s past. With the help of others, Gulnisa escaped again this time leaving the country. She fled to Iran, hoping for safety. But even there, her struggles continued. Her stepchildren followed her and threatened her again. Now, she lives in hiding, without stability. Her children cannot go to school and must work to survive. Despite everything she has faced forced marriage, violence, loss, and displacement Gulnisa continues to move forward. Her story is one of survival in the face of constant hardship.

Sajida’s Journey

Sajida, a 40-year-old Afghan woman, shares her life of abuse, survival, and her quiet struggle to protect her children in a world that gives her no support.

Sajida’s Journey

Sajida is a 40-year-old woman whose life has been shaped by pain, silence, and survival. The sadness in her eyes tells a story that words can barely carry. The woman who once laughed freely now lives with deep grief that never leaves her. Her marriage began with hope but quickly turned into suffering. She married to escape the hard life of her village and believed her husband would give her a better future. She had never seen him before the marriage, but she trusted that her life would improve. Instead, her new life became even harder. From the beginning, she was forced to do heavy work in the house washing clothes by hand, carrying water, and managing everything alone. But the hardest part was not the work. It was the violence. Her husband beat her again and again, often without reason. Sometimes the pain was so severe that she lost consciousness. He only stopped when he became tired. The physical wounds slowly healed, but the emotional pain stayed. For years, Sajida stayed silent. She hoped that one day her husband would change. She believed that maybe their life could become better. But that hope slowly disappeared. Her husband was not only violent he was also irresponsible. He gambled away everything they had. He sold her jewelry, their belongings, and even the basic things they needed to live. When she tried to stop him, he beat her again and told her she had no right to question him. Despite all this, Sajida continued to survive for her children. She gave birth to four children and raised them almost alone. There were days when they had only dry bread to eat, and sometimes not even that. To keep them alive, she worked in other people’s homes, washing clothes and doing small jobs. Then one day, her husband disappeared after the Taliban returned to power. Instead of relief, her problems became even worse. As a woman alone in a strict society, she now faces constant fear. People come to her house asking about her husband and demanding answers. His creditors threaten her and her children. Some even warn they might kidnap her children. She lives every day in fear, trying to protect her family with no support. Sajida says, “I stayed silent for years, and now I have nowhere to speak. My life may end, but my sorrow will not.” Her story is not only her own. It reflects the reality of many women who live in silence, fear, and pressure. Women who are expected to endure everything without speaking, without support, and without justice. Still, despite everything, Sajida continues to stand for her children, and for survival.

Marzia’s Journey

Marzia, a former professional and breadwinner, shares her journey of losing her work, her independence, and her sense of stability and her struggle to survive and hold onto hope.

Marzia’s Journey

Marzia was once a woman full of dreams and purpose. She studied engineering and journalism, worked in society, and supported not only herself but her entire family. She was active, hopeful, and deeply connected to her community. Today, her life looks completely different. After the Taliban banned women from working, Marzia lost her job. As the only provider for her eight-member family, this change affected every part of her life. Now, she faces serious financial hardship and struggles to meet even basic needs. Her days are no longer filled with learning, working, and helping others. Instead, she spends her time at home, trying to cope with growing pressure and emotional pain. Sometimes, she picks up a book about depression, searching for something that might help her understand her own feelings. She says, “After the ban, I was forced to stay home. This has deeply affected both my mental health and my life. I have been unemployed for more than three years. Before, I was active and independent. Now, I am struggling with many problems.” Marzia remembers how women and girls protested, hoping for change. They raised their voices again and again, asking for their basic rights. But their efforts brought little result. Now, she calls on the world not to ignore their situation. She asks the international community to take action and support Afghan women in regaining their rights to work, education, and dignity. Her story is not only personal it reflects a much larger crisis. When women are not allowed to work, families suffer. Poverty increases. Children lose opportunities. Society becomes weaker. Many women, like Marzia, are now trapped in a cycle of dependence, isolation, and emotional struggle. Some women say they feel completely stuck, with no way forward. The loss of work and education has not only created economic problems but also deep psychological pain. Marzia continues to live in this reality holding onto what remains of her strength, while hoping for a future where she and others can return to a normal life.

Story of a Girl Who Refused to Be Silenced

A 19-year-old Afghan girl shares her journey of living under restrictions while continuing to learn, teach, and stay connected to society.

Story of a Girl Who Refused to Be Silenced

She is 19 years old, originally from Herat, now living in Kabul. She studies, teaches English, and works with organizations. Like many girls, she faced major challenges when schools were closed for girls. She also experiences strict social control in public spaces. She describes how she is often watched and judged, even questioned for simple things like sitting in a car with her brother. These experiences make her feel constantly observed. At times, she feels her voice is muted not only in society but sometimes in decision-making spaces. She recalls a moment when she and other girls asked questions in a learning center. They raised concerns about disrespectful teaching, but their voices were ignored. They were told to accept the situation or leave. That moment made her realize how easily girls’ voices can be dismissed. She believes fear of judgment is one of the main reasons girls do not share their stories. They want to appear strong and hide their struggles. What helps her stay strong is support from family, friends, and staying connected to society through courses and activities. Her message is not only about suffering but about resilience. Afghan girls are facing challenges, but they are still continuing and not giving up. They are still learning, teaching, and trying to find ways to be heard. A Voice That Refuses to Disappear

She is a 16-year-old student studying through a virtual academy in Herat. She is passionate about learning and hopes to continue her education. The biggest challenge she has faced is being deprived of her right to education. This does not only silence her—it makes her feel like she does not exist. She strongly believes education, society, and media shape how girls’ voices are heard. Education builds confidence, society can silence or support, and media can either highlight or hide their struggles. What keeps her strong is her hope for the future and her belief that her voice still matters—even if it is not heard right now. She believes society must start truly listening to girls and giving them equal rights, especially education. She hopes that by sharing her story, other girls will feel less afraid and more confident to speak.

inspire & learn

Every story in this project carries a real experience. These are not just stories they are lessons about strength, silence, hope, and survival. By listening to these voices, we can better understand what Afghan girls go through and what truly needs to change.

  • Education is more than learning it is protection:
    Many girls in these stories describe education as their only way to build confidence, think freely, and defend their future.
  • Silence is often created by fear:
    Several girls shared that they stay quiet not because they have nothing to say, but because of judgment, pressure, and lack of safe spaces.
  • Strength can be quiet:
    Not every girl fights loudly. Some show strength by continuing their studies, supporting their families, and not giving up even in silence.
  • Support can change everything:
    In many stories, even one supportive person a parent, friend, or teacher made a big difference in helping a girl stay strong.
  • Many girls are forced to grow up too early:
    Some experiences show how girls take on adult responsibilities and pressures long before they are ready.
  • Feeling unheard is very common:
    Across different stories, many girls feel their ideas are ignored at home, in school, and in society.
  • Hope still exists, even in hard conditions:
    Despite challenges, many girls continue to believe in a better future and keep working toward it.
  • Girls are not only facing problems they are building solutions:
    Some are teaching, learning online, leading small projects, and helping others despite limitations.
  • Change starts with listening, not speaking for others:
    These stories show that real understanding begins when we listen directly to girls instead of speaking on their behalf.
  • Every voice matters:
    Each story no matter how simple has the power to inspire, raise awareness, and create change.

research & insights

This section shares what we learned from real stories, interviews, and survey responses. We collected answers from 14 participants and explored common patterns in their experiences.

After reviewing all the stories and responses, a few clear themes appeared:

  • Limited access to education:
    Many girls said education is restricted, which affects their confidence and future opportunities.
  • Fear of judgment:
    Girls often stay silent because they are afraid of being judged by family or society.
  • Voices not taken seriously:
    Several participants shared that their opinions are ignored or seen as less important.
  • Lack of safe spaces:
    Many girls feel there is no safe place where they can speak freely.
  • Strong resilience:
    Despite challenges, girls continue learning, working, and trying to build a better future.

Survey responses also showed that most participants believe girls do not have enough opportunities to share their voices.

Ethics & Safety

Our Responsibility

This project shares real experiences, so protecting people is our first priority. We make sure every story is shared in a safe, respectful, and responsible way.

  • All stories are shared anonymously to protect identity
  • No real names, exact locations, or identifying details are included
  • Sensitive information is carefully adjusted when needed to ensure safety
  • Clear consent is received before using any story
  • We avoid sharing any content that could put individuals at risk in their communities.

We understand that these stories are personal and sometimes painful. Our goal is not just to share them, but to do it in a way that respects each voice and keeps every contributor safe.
